2019 09 08 Gapminder a New Hope

Clément VIOLET
Sep 8, 2019 4 min read

Hi! This week was important to me, because I am having a fresh start, so it was important to me to pay a tribute to someone I really admire: Hans Roseling.

A quick recap onto his biography: Hans Roseling was a Swedish MD and more important to me, a statistician (in fact he was an epidemiologist, but they are doing a lot of stats) and a sword swallower. He started a foundation which aims to help people to a better understanding of statistics.

One day during my BSc, I have founded one his Ted talk titled: The best stats you’ve ever seen. This talk changed the way that I am looking at the world. It has made me more optimistic about future, even if we have a tremendous number of challenges to face off. If you have not watched one of his talks, stop everything and watch it!



His conference totaly blowed my mind. If you liked it too, you should watch one of his documentary: Don’t Panic - The Truth about Population.

Since there I know that I want to use stats to better understand our world. Sure, I still passionate with marine sciences. That’s why I want to use statistical methods to get insight into how the marine communities work.

This week, I am starting, a new Master’s degree in Ecological Modelling. I would like to thank Hans Roseling, what a better way than using some of Gapminder’s data and making cool animated graphics?

So let’s do it! I have already tidy a new dataset based on the brilliant Jennifer Bryan gapminder R package. It will be the occasion to show off some gganimate features!

First of all, I load some convinient libraries and my own version of gapminder dataset.

library(tidyverse)
library(gganimate)

This new dataset has two new variables: * childMort the number of deaths of children under 5 years per 1,000 births; * fertility the number of children per woman.

Let’s do some animated graphs! First, I want to plot one of the most famous animated plots, let’s start with life expectancy against the income.

p_1 <- ggplot(gapminder, aes(x = gdpPercap, y = lifeExp)) +
  geom_point(aes(color = continent, size = pop)) +
  scale_color_manual(name = "Continent", values = gapminder::continent_colors) +
  scale_size_continuous(guide = FALSE) + # I remove the legend for the size, because of gif animation.
  scale_x_log10() +
  labs(x = "log10($ GDP per capita)",
       y = "# Children per women",
       title = paste("Year:", "{round(frame_time, 0)}")) +
  transition_time(year)

animate(p_1, fps = 5)

What’s about the the fertility against the income?

Fantastic! The fertility keep decreasing and decreasing! Are we still expecting the Population Bomb? Now let’s look at the life expentency and child mortality?
